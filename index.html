Mammals

The region of mammals and the species within them is a diverse and complex topic. Mammals are classified into three main groups: Monotremes, Marsupials, and Placentals, each with unique characteristics and habitats. For example, Monotremes include the platypus and echidna, Marsupials like kangaroos and koalas, and Placentals such as mice and whales. These groups represent the evolutionary diversity of mammals, with Placentals being the most diverse group. Mammals inhabit nearly every corner of the globe, from forests and grasslands to deserts and wetlands. Each region supports unique mammalian species, reflecting the adaptability and evolutionary history of this class of animals. For instance, North America's habitats include forests, grasslands, deserts, and wetlands, each supporting a variety of mammalian species. The concept of zoogeographical realms, introduced by P. L. Sclater and Alfred Russel Wallace, provides a framework for understanding the distribution of various species across the planet. These realms, such as the Palaearctic, Nearctic, and Australian, are defined by the presence or absence of specific organisms and are characterized by distinctive barriers that separate each realm from the others. Understanding the distribution patterns of mammals across these realms is crucial for conservation efforts and the appreciation of the biodiversity that these species represent.
